# The Art of Loving

> Notes from Erich Fromm's *The Art of Loving*, partly shaped by discussions around *See You Again* (*再见爱人*). Personal reflections and doubts are kept where they matter.

## 1. Three Common Misunderstandings about Love

People often get love wrong in three ways:

1. **Love as being loved**  
   Most people ask how to *be* loved, not how to love—or whether they have the capacity to love.

2. **Love as finding the right object**  
   Love is reduced to “finding the right person,” as if the right match would make love happen by itself.

3. **Falling in love as love**  
   Passion, infatuation, and butterflies are not what Fromm means by love. They fade; love is a sustained practice.

Love is not finding someone and then feeling love. It is **creating and receiving love through action**.

## 2. Mature Love: Union without Losing the Self

A hobby is meaningful when it is creative and helps you connect with the world around you. If it only distracts you or becomes addictive escape, it is not a real hobby.

Love points toward a **union of two people**—but not a union without dignity. Masochism and sadism do not count.

> Mature love is a union that preserves one’s dignity and individuality: the capacity to overcome the barriers between people.

Without resolving the capacity to love and be loved, a relationship rarely lasts.

## 3. Love Is an Activity, Not a Mood

Love is an **activity**: an ongoing active practice, not a passive emotion you happen to feel.

- **Passive action** is driven by external motives—for money, for face, or to avoid responsibility.
- **Active action** is free: you are the master of what you do. Virtue and ability are essentially the same; active behavior is the expression of ability.

### Everyday Avoidance

In household division of labor, people often say: “I cooperate, I just can’t do it well.” If you know you could take initiative and still refuse (e.g. chores), that is not an act of love—it is **avoiding the responsibility of love**.

The same applies to planning a wedding: once you agree, you cannot sit back because “they wanted it.” You may clearly object; once consensus is reached, what you promised deserves real effort. Marriage and partnership are a joint body—after compromise or agreement, you own the outcome.

## 4. Giving: The Core of Love

Love is giving, not taking. It is a creative activity you can immerse yourself in—and find joy in the giving. Wealth is not how much you own, but **whether you can give**. Extreme poverty can strip away the capacity to give.

Real giving is natural and spontaneous. Being a giver does not mean you receive nothing: the vitality you awaken in others often comes back to you.

### Two Kinds of Help

1. **Real help** sparks another person’s creativity—explaining principles so they can grow.
2. **False help** only tells them what to do and expects obedience—that is not real help.

The same is true for teachers and students. If teaching is only exam drilling, the teacher’s own sense of fulfillment stays low. Love is the **capacity to create love**; waiting to be loved makes mutual love unlikely.

Gifts work the same way: price matters less than whether you heard what they casually wished for. If they already said they do not care much about food, insisting on a fancy dinner may be feeding your own need to give, not their need.

### Listening

When someone shares their troubles, do not rush to offer solutions, and do not say “I already advised you—you just wouldn’t listen.” Often what they need is a hug and presence. Love is **active action not driven by ulterior motives**.

## 5. The Four Elements of Love

Fromm names four elements of love: **care, responsibility, respect, and knowledge**.

| Element | Meaning |
|---------|---------|
| **Care** | Active concern for the life and growth of the beloved; seeing what they truly want |
| **Responsibility** | A voluntary response, not an external duty; not merely “doing your part” (that is reliability), but responding to need |
| **Respect** | Seeing the person’s unique individuality as they are—not turning them into a tool for your ends |
| **Knowledge** | Only real understanding teaches you how to love and respond; it cannot stay on the surface |

Knowledge is also a human need for existence. Extreme sadism tries to “know” someone by control and pain—turning a person into a thing. Real knowledge cannot come from observation alone; it grows through practice and union with the other.

Love is exclusive; romantic love is not unconditional love. If your loving acts never awaken love in return, love in Fromm’s fuller sense is not yet complete.

## 6. Immature Love and Mature Love

- **Immature love**: I love because I am loved.
- **Mature love**: I am loved because I love.

“Unconditional love,” when it becomes an unchangeable cage, is a prison. Conditional love means we can create love through our actions.

Can someone who does not love you now come to love you later? Possibly—because love is active giving. What matters is whether they are willing to give, and whether they act.

## 7. Marriage, Commitment, and Effort

Marriage is a serious decision: a **commitment to give love and to act lovingly**. Entering a relationship means entering that commitment. If one side cannot keep it and problems cannot be solved, parting is allowed.

But parting at the first difficulty is not the point. Both sides should first try their hardest. Love always meets obstacles; seeking professional help when needed is part of the work.

Effort in love includes not only your own giving, but also **giving the other time to choose**—with a clear deadline.

“Marry first, love later” is not impossible: many people grow real love through shared life.

### On Infidelity and Family

The book suggests that if there is sincere remorse, deep communication, and rebuilt trust, a relationship can continue.

> I still find this hard to fully accept—can love really go that far? Even imagining it feels difficult.

A complete family filled with tension and misery can hurt a child more than a broken one. Divorce can also teach a child that people may decisively end an unbearable situation.

A loving mother’s task includes accepting the wish for separation, and continuing to love after it. Maternal love moves from fusion toward separation; romantic love moves from separation toward fusion. Education should help children realize their potential—including the capacity to love.

## 8. Loving Yourself: A Baseline, Not Selfishness

Love includes loving yourself and loving others. Loving only yourself is not love; “selflessness” that erases the self is not love either.

Loving yourself is a baseline in relationships—it helps you avoid toxic traps. The four elements apply to yourself too:

- **Care**: do not let work consume all of life; make room for comfort.
- **Responsibility**: do not neglect your own spiritual needs.
- **Respect / knowledge**: recognize and respond to what you truly need.

## 9. Practicing the Capacity to Love

1. **Give yourself structure**  
   For example, cook a set number of times each week—sustained action helps you through the hard beginning and builds the capacity to be alone.

2. **Patience, courage, and faith**  
   Action takes courage. To grow the capacity to love, first believe that love exists, and hold the hope that your love can awaken love in return.

3. **Test by vitality**  
   If a relationship makes you more alive, it is closer to real love; if not, be cautious. Fitness and similar practices also build the capacity to love—energy makes creative action possible. Real love creates vitality and supports growth.

## 10. In One Sentence

Love is a need for existence: active, generous giving, made of care, responsibility, respect, and knowledge. Real love is hard, because many people never learned how in their family of origin—so two people must keep working at it.

Love is not the emotion of being moved by someone. It is an active struggle for the growth and happiness of the beloved.
