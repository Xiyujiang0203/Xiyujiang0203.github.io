# Li Zhonggan Career Insights

> Li Zhonggan graduated with a bachelor's degree in Computer Science and Technology from Xiamen University in 2010. He worked at Huawei Technologies for 8 years, serving in roles including product marketing for product lines, regional data center solution sales director, and regional computing business director. In September 2021, he served as Deputy General Manager of Fujian at xFusion Digital Technologies Co., Ltd. for 2 years. Since September 2023, he has been a senior partner at Xiamen Huiqiao Investment Co., Ltd. and Shanghai Qipu Investment Management Co., Ltd., deeply involved in multiple investment projects and listed company M&A projects, achieving high valuation returns from software investments. In August 2025, he founded industrial cluster companies including Union Star (Xiamen) Technology Co., Ltd. and Yuanqi Innovation (Xiamen) Robotics Co., Ltd., which developed rapidly and built core capabilities in AI software development, intelligent robot application technology solution development and sales, robot scenario-based data collection and operations, and robot peripheral derivative development and sales. He has extensive front-line sales and management experience, exceptionally strong resource integration capabilities, and regional industry operation capabilities. In May 2026, Yuanqi Innovation was designated as the "Official Robot Service Provider" at the 26th China International Fair for Investment & Trade (CIFIT).

## 1. On Entrepreneurship, Investment, and Ecosystem Partnerships

### Context

The prior discussion covered whether startups should accept investment and how to manage relationships with platform companies or ecosystem partners.

### Summary of Responses

The speaker believes that capital is inherently profit-driven. When a company is performing well, investors typically offer support; but once problems arise, investors must answer first to LPs and other contributors, so they tend to exit quickly and may even add pressure on founders. For people who are truly building a business, capital is sometimes not a boost but a constraint.

Therefore, he prefers to first turn a startup into a profitable business, and only then consider shaping it into an investment project better suited for fundraising, M&A, or acquisition. In other words, startups should solve the business loop first, rather than designing the company around fundraising logic from day one.

He also noted that if a company is fully controlled by a large platform or institution, founders struggle to retain real control—equity stakes and decision-making power are both constrained. During entrepreneurship, you need to balance "leveraging ecosystem resources" with "preserving company independence."

In robotics and embodied intelligence, large platforms hold leading positions but still need many ecosystem partners. Opportunities for startups often lie not in building core large components already covered by the platform, but in joining its ecosystem to fill finer, smaller, more specific scenarios—for example, providing real mechanical scenarios, data, and product validation environments.

## 2. What Skills Should Students Focus on for Job Hunting?

### Question

With the job market getting tougher, what abilities, qualities, or experience do employers value most when students seek jobs?

### Summary of Responses

The speaker believes hiring starts with a person's character. "Character" here is not vague moral judgment, but whether someone is grounded, restless or steady, and willing to truly get things done.

He mentioned that the company currently hires relatively few fresh graduates; core roles more often go to people with several years of experience, especially those with relevant ICT industry experience who understand products, markets, customers, and ecosystems. The business involves resource integration, customer needs, industry ecosystems, and project delivery—not just writing code or building standalone products.

In terms of staffing structure, the company roughly uses three types of people:

1. Outsourced or dispatched workers, mainly for foundational, standardized, scalable work such as data collection operations and maintenance.
2. Interns, mainly for data collection and related tasks.
3. Core in-house employees, held to higher standards—typically at least a bachelor's degree—with greater emphasis on overall capability and growth potential.

He stressed that striving does not mean mechanically working overtime every day, but continuously thinking through what you are responsible for. Everyone should actively consider how to improve their own business area, not just passively respond. If most of the team only executes passively, it becomes "one person moving forward while a group pulls backward."

For recent graduates, he offered several suggestions:

- First clarify your career direction: market, frontend, backend, algorithms, product, brand, and other paths differ greatly.
- Then assess whether the target company can offer growth opportunities—not just company prestige.
- For most new graduates, starting at a large company or a more mature platform is a good choice.
- Large companies have complex processes, many rules, and intricate interpersonal and business relationships—but precisely because of that complexity, they better train your ability to break down problems, coordinate resources, and drive projects forward.

He believes personal capability is not demonstrated through talk, but accumulated through "every matter followed through, every item closed out." Customers truly trust someone not because they drink well or speak well, but because they can actually solve critical problems.

## 3. How to Choose Among Technical, Sales, and Product Skills?

### Context

Students wonder whether to pursue technology, marketing, product, or sales after graduation.

### Summary of Responses

The speaker believes no direction is absolutely better or worse, but you must choose a path and go deep over the long term. Do not want technology today, sales tomorrow, and product the day after. Short-term returns may not be obvious, but if you keep doing each task well, capability accumulates gradually.

If you choose the technical route, polish your technical skills like a craftsman over the long haul. But he also cautions: even in technology, pay attention to whether the technology is "useful." If technology cannot connect to industry needs, customer needs, and market needs, it may end up stuck in the lab or self-appreciation stage.

If you choose sales, you cannot rely only on drinking, socializing, and carrying bags. Modern sales requires understanding products, technology, and customer businesses. Especially in ICT, robotics, and AI industries, customers do not respect people who only maintain relationships but do not understand the business. Truly valuable salespeople are technical salespeople or solution-oriented salespeople.

If you aim for management later, whether from a technical or sales background, you need to round out market, product, customer, and industry understanding. At leadership level, single-point skills are no longer enough—you must connect technology, product, market, and delivery.

## 4. City Choice: Why Recommend Big Cities?

### Context

The speaker continued on city choice from a job hunting and career development perspective.

### Summary of Responses

The speaker believes that if you want to grow in business, enterprise, and industry directions, you should try to go to developed, vibrant major cities. The reason is that major cities offer more industry opportunities, more complete platforms, denser talent pools, and faster information flow.

He mentioned that in his view, Shenzhen is an economically vibrant city, especially strong in electronics manufacturing, robotics, and hardware industry chains. Beijing is stronger in large models, research, and policy resources; Shanghai has universities, algorithm resources, and the Yangtze River Delta's traditional manufacturing base—especially automotive and robotics industries that draw heavily on manufacturing resources. So Shanghai also offers good opportunities.

By comparison, new first-tier cities like Chengdu and Chongqing have certain advantages, especially at the automotive, machinery, defense, and manufacturing foundation level. But for application layers, model layers, new startup opportunities, and capital-intensive opportunities, coastal cities generally offer more.

The speaker also noted that a person's state is influenced by the city environment. If you stay long in a slow-paced, low-opportunity, not particularly ambitious environment, your personal growth can be dragged down. Conversely, if the people around you are actively working, learning new technology, and exchanging new opportunities, you are more likely to stay in an upward state.

But he also clarified that this advice mainly targets people who want to pursue business, enterprise, and industry paths. If your goal is civil service, the public sector, or stable development back home, the decision logic differs—you cannot judge by the same standard.

## 5. Do Different Majors Have Opportunities in Robotics and Embodied Intelligence?

### Context

A student mentioned they study Digital Media Technology—a field related to computer science, but not a traditional computer science major.

### Summary of Responses

The speaker believes robotics and embodied intelligence will involve many disciplines in the future, not only computer science, mechanical engineering, or automation. The reason is that robots are essentially doing "what humans can do." Any task humans can complete may eventually be attempted by robots.

Therefore, the robotics track will need diverse capabilities:

- Computer science and algorithms;
- Mechanical, control, and sensor engineering;
- Data collection, data operations, and scenario design;
- Product, delivery, market, and customer need understanding;
- Vision, interaction, content, and digital media-related capabilities.

So majors like Digital Media Technology and Digital Media Arts, if combined with computer science, interaction, vision, data, or product directions, may also find a place in the robotics and embodied intelligence industry.

## 6. Choose the Track or the Company?

### Question

If you face two types of choices: a large company whose business may not be on the cutting edge, or a mid-sized company in embodied intelligence with strong growth potential—how do you choose between "track" and "company"?

### Summary of Responses

The speaker said that for himself, he would prioritize the track—but ideally at an upper-mid-tier company in a good track, or at least a platform with a solid position in the industry. The reason is that once you enter a good track, there are more opportunities within the industry; future job moves, growth, and resource accumulation all go more smoothly.

When hiring for robotics roles, he looks at whether candidates have relevant robotics experience. Even if he has not heard of the candidate's company before, as long as that company is solid in a niche track—such as commercial cleaning robots, systems integration, or AI—he will consider them seriously.

But if the so-called "large company" is a major player in sunrise industries like internet, IT, AI, or robotics, he still considers it worth prioritizing. Conversely, if it is a very traditional company with stagnant business, even at large scale, he does not recommend young people stay long-term—stagnant business can wear people down.

In summary:

> The priority is not simply "large company is always better" or "startup is always better." First look at whether the track has growth potential, then look at the company's position and platform strength within that track.

## 7. Who Is Suited for Long-Term Development at Huawei or Similar Large Companies?

### Question

You worked at Huawei—what kind of people adapt better to Huawei culture, or find it easier to grow there?

### Summary of Responses

The speaker believes the first type is people who get things done. "Getting things done" means not just personal effort, but ultimately delivering results.

The second type is people with strong communication and coordination skills. At Huawei or similar large companies, organizational structures are complex, with many departments and long processes. Many things cannot be completed by one person or one department alone—they require upward reporting, lateral collaboration, and cross-department driving. Huawei internally often uses a term called "拉通" (lā tōng, "alignment"); internet giants have similar concepts.

"Alignment capability" means:

- Connecting relevant departments, roles, and resources;
- Understanding different departments' business processes;
- Driving others to cooperate toward your goals;
- Pushing things forward amid complex relationships.

He believes most people are not genius-level technical experts and cannot rely on single-point technical skills for long-term growth. For ordinary professionals, coordination, reporting, communication, resource integration, and project driving skills are very important.

The third type is people with drive who do not avoid problems. If you instinctively want to hide when difficulties arise, even if you attend meetings and speak up on the surface, you cannot actually deliver value. In a large company, whether someone truly solves problems eventually becomes clear to everyone.

## 8. How Are Chengdu, Chongqing, and Other New First-Tier Cities Developing in Robotics and Embodied Intelligence?

### Question

Drawing on government, enterprise, and university resources, how do you view the development of new first-tier cities like Chengdu and Chongqing in embodied intelligence and robotics?

### Summary of Responses

The speaker believes Chengdu and Chongqing have a solid industrial foundation, especially in automotive, machinery, and some defense-related industries, with a certain manufacturing base. So in the robotics industry chain, the backend, manufacturing, and foundation layers offer opportunities in Chengdu and Chongqing.

But for application layers, model layers, new business models, startup opportunities, and capital-intensive opportunities, he believes coastal cities are stronger overall. Chengdu is among the more vibrant cities in central-western or southwestern China; if students come from inland regions, developing in Chengdu may be a relatively suitable choice.

He still emphasized that city environment affects personal state. The more active the industry, the denser the talent, the faster information flows—the easier it is for young people to stay in a learning and striving mindset.

## 9. How to Uncover Market Demand?

### Question

If we want to build a product and sell it, how do we find products customers are willing to accept and use?

### Summary of Responses

The speaker believes B2B and B2C products follow different logic. He focuses more on B2B products; after many years at Huawei, he often "secured the order first, then built the product"—meaning clear customer demand comes first, then product development is driven backward from that.

He calls this industry thinking or sales thinking:

> Secure market demand or orders first, then determine what product to develop.

Entrepreneurs from a sales background often start from market and orders, so their probability of commercial success may be higher. But this path also has drawbacks: the technology may not be the most advanced, because it prioritizes real demand over the most cutting-edge lab technology.

He mentioned two routes—"trade-industry-technology" (贸工技) and "technology-trade-industry" (技贸工)—and believes that if the goal is to build a good business, you usually start with trade-industry-technology:

1. Solve market and customer needs first;
2. Then determine what product to build and how to operate;
3. After stable cash flow, reinvest part of profits into R&D;
4. Gradually upgrade technical capability.

He believes Huawei followed a similar path early on: start with distribution and sales, then gradually build its own products such as switches, and continuously accumulate technical capability.

He is cautious about starting a company right after university graduation. At that stage, industry resources, networks, customer trust, and team capability are usually insufficient, so success probability is typically low. If you first accumulate several years of experience at a large company or in an industry—building customers, ecosystem partners, industry understanding, and team foundations—starting a business is more realistic.

He also stressed that a startup team does not necessarily need many people at first, but the core team must be reliable. A few core partners are like locomotives that can pull an entire train forward; if core team values and ways of working diverge, everything becomes very difficult later.

## 10. From Huawei to Entrepreneurship: How to Navigate Career Planning and Track Selection?

### Question

You worked at Huawei early on and later started your own business—a large career span. How did you approach career planning and track selection?

### Summary of Responses

The speaker believes that for ordinary individuals, it is hard to see all future opportunities clearly. A more realistic approach is to pay attention to era trends and national strategic directions. What the country encourages usually brings policy, state capital, industry resources, social capital, and talent flow—factors that jointly push a track forward.

He mentioned that career development should try to "resonate in sync" with national strategic direction. That sounds macro, but the practical meaning is:

- See what national policy prioritizes;
- See where capital and industry resources are flowing;
- See where new technology, new models, and new demand are emerging;
- Once you enter a relevant track, keep running—opportunities will gradually emerge along the way.

He used his own entry into robotics and embodied intelligence as an example: before going deep, he only knew about it through the internet; but once committed, as policy, capital, technology direction, and business opportunities kept appearing, more and more new opportunities surfaced.

Looking back at his Huawei experience, he sees Huawei itself as a matrix organization with many choices—each role and regional assignment matters. When he first joined Huawei, he considered overseas posts such as South America and Brazil, because overseas roles offered separation and hardship allowances with potentially higher income. But he chose to stay in China, partly because domestic IT, de-IOE, internet, real estate, and other opportunities were developing rapidly at the time.

Later inside Huawei he also kept choosing new tracks: from networking to IT, then to cloud computing, localization, large models, and AI. Behind each shift were national policy, industry trends, and customer needs.

His core lesson:

> Career planning is not a one-time plan to the end. Under a broadly correct direction, keep observing trends, choosing growth tracks, and accumulating capability and resources at each stage.

## III. Key Takeaways Summary

1. Build a profitable business first, then consider fundraising and capitalization.
2. Capital is not inherently benevolent—founders should guard against losing real control.
3. For job hunting, choose your direction first, then assess whether the platform offers growth opportunities.
4. The value of large companies is not just salary, but training in coordination, process, and project driving within complex systems.
5. Whether in technology, sales, or product, you must ultimately solve real problems.
6. Sales cannot rely only on relationship maintenance—you must understand products, technology, and customer business.
7. Technology cannot stay at "showing off skills"—it must connect to industry, market, and application scenarios.
8. City choice affects opportunity density and personal state; for business and industry paths, prioritize vibrant major cities.
9. A good track matters more than company name alone—but ideally enter a quality platform within a good track.
10. One of the most important skills in large companies is "alignment": cross-department communication, resource coordination, and driving results.
11. B2B products are better built from real customer demand and orders, not in isolation.
12. Starting a business right after graduation usually has low success probability—accumulating industry resources, customer resources, and team experience first is more prudent.
13. Career planning should follow national strategy, industry trends, and long-term growth tracks.
14. Embodied intelligence and robotics will need multi-disciplinary fusion—not only traditional computer science or mechanical engineering.

## IV. Actionable Advice for Students

### 1. Clarify Your Direction First

Do not only ask "which role pays more" or "which company is more famous." First judge whether you fit technology, product, market, sales, delivery, algorithms, data, operations, or brand.

### 2. Try to Enter Growth Tracks

If you can enter growth tracks such as AI, robotics, embodied intelligence, cloud computing, localization, or advanced manufacturing—even if the starting point is not the top-tier large company—long-term opportunities may be better.

### 3. Build Closed-Loop Capability

Every task should have feedback, progress, and results. Workplace trust is not built through talk, but through consistent delivery over time.

### 4. Improve "Alignment" Skills

Learn to communicate with different departments and roles, break complex matters apart, coordinate resources, and drive goals to completion.

### 5. Do Not Chase the Startup Halo Too Early

Entrepreneurship is not "have an idea, then build a product." You need customer demand, industry resources, team capability, and a business loop. As a student, you can first accumulate industry knowledge and real project experience.
